1. Concept Overview (VLAN Pruning)
By default, a Cisco trunk interface allows all VLANs (1-4094) to pass traffic across the link.
However, forwarding unnecessary broadcast traffic to switches that do not have active ports in those VLANs wastes bandwidth and compromises security.

To optimize network efficiency, we configure VLAN Pruning manually using the (switchport trunk allowed vlan) command.
  This limits the trunk link to carrying only the specific VLAN traffic required by the neighboring switches.  
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
 2. Practical Configuration Steps 
Option A: Allowing Only Specific VLANs (Overwriting the list)

To configure and manage allowed VLANs on a trunk interface, navigate to the specific trunk port (e.g., FastEthernet 0/1):
If you want to restrict the trunk to only permit specific VLANs (e.g., VLAN 10 and VLAN 20) and block everything else:

Switch(config)# interface f0/1
Switch(config-if)# switchport trunk allowed vlan 10,20
Note:This command completely rewrites the allowed list. Only VLAN 10 and 20 can cross this link now; all other VLANs are implicitly denied.  

Option B: Adding a New VLAN to an Existing Allowed List

If the trunk is already filtering traffic, and you want to add a new VLAN (e.g., VLAN 30) without erasing your previous configuration (VLAN 10 and 20):

Switch(config-if)# switchport trunk allowed vlan add 30
Warning for learners: Always use the "add" keyword when modifying an existing list.
  If you type switchport trunk allowed vlan 30 without add, it will wipe out VLAN 10 and 20 from the trunk.  

Option C: Denying/Removing a Specific VLAN from the Trunk
If you want to explicitly block or remove a specific VLAN (e.g., VLAN 10) from passing through the trunk link:

Switch(config-if)# switchport trunk allowed vlan remove 10

Option D: Resetting to Factory Defaults (Allow All)
To remove all restrictions and allow all VLANs to pass through the trunk interface once again:

Switch(config-if)# no switchport trunk allowed vlan
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

3. Verification Commands
To verify which VLANs are currently permitted to cross your trunk interface, return to Privileged EXEC mode (Switch#) and run:

Switch# show interfaces trunk

## 4. The Engineering Philosophy: Why Do We Need This Command?

A common question among learners is: **"Why did network engineers design this command, and what real-world problem does it solve?"**

When networks began growing into hundreds of nodes, engineers faced two critical bottlenecks: **Network Degradation (Performance)** and **Information Exposure (Security)**.

---

### Problem 1: The Wasteful Broadcast Traffic (Performance)
By default, **Broadcast frames** (like ARP requests) are sent to every single device inside a VLAN. 

* **The Scenario:** Imagine a university campus with two switches connected by a trunk link. 
  * `Switch A` has active ports in `VLAN 10` (Students) and `VLAN 50` (Admission Office).
  * `Switch B` only has student devices (`VLAN 10`) and has **zero** ports assigned to `VLAN 50`.
* **Without VLAN Pruning:** If a device in `VLAN 50` on `Switch A` sends a broadcast message, the switch automatically forwards it across the Trunk link to `Switch B`. `Switch B` receives the frame, processes it, realizes it has no ports in `VLAN 50`, and drops it.
* **The Engineering Solution:** This process wastes valuable trunk bandwidth and unnecessary CPU cycles on `Switch B`. By configuring `switchport trunk allowed vlan`, engineers created a "filter gateway" at the entrance of the trunk link to stop unwanted broadcast traffic from travelling to switches that do not need it.

---

### Problem 2: Minimizing the Attack Surface (Security)
In networking, we follow the **Principle of Least Privilege** (giving devices only the access they strictly need to function).

* **The Risk:** If a Trunk link permits all VLANs by default, an attacker who compromises a switch downstream could potentially capture or sniff sensitive control and data frames belonging to management or financial VLANs—even if those VLANs were never meant to exist on that specific switch.
* **The Engineering Solution:** Manually restricting the allowed VLANs on a trunk link ensures that sensitive data never leaves the core switches unless explicitly required. If an attacker snoops on a restricted trunk link, the data for unauthorized VLANs is physically non-existent on that wire.

## 6. Real-World Troubleshooting Note: The "Two-Way Street" Rule

During the practical implementation of this lab, a critical behavior was observed regarding how packet forwarding fails when configurations are mismatched.

### The Observation:
If you restrict or block a VLAN on **Switch A's** trunk port but leave it allowed on **Switch B's** opposing trunk port, any host in that VLAN attempting to reach the other side will receive a **`Request Timed Out`** error during a `ping` test.

### Why does this happen?
Network communication is always a **two-way street** consisting of a request and a reply (e.g., ICMP Echo Request and ICMP Echo Reply). 

1. **Traffic Dropped at the Source Gateway:** When a host on Switch A tries to ping Switch B, Switch A checks its local trunk configuration list. Since the VLAN is blocked on its interface, Switch A drops the packet immediately (`Drop`). The packet never leaves the switch.
2. **The Destination Never Responds:** Because the packet is dropped at the very first gateway, it never reaches Switch B. Switch B cannot reply to a message it never received, resulting in a timeout.

---

### Key Takeaway for Learners:
> 💡 **Trunk configuration must be symmetric!** For a Trunk link to function correctly, both opposing interfaces on both switches must have **identical allowed/denied VLAN policies**. A mismatch on even one side will break the entire communication path.
